# Open World Assembly Canon

## Definition and aim

Open World Assembly (OWA) is a decentralized, fractal system of direct democracy intended to let people coordinate, deliberate, and make decisions together at any scale without relying on centralized power or permanent political intermediaries. It is designed as a parallel democratic layer, open and consent-based, that reflects the perspectives and visible consent of participants directly.

OWA’s core intent is practical civic alignment: enabling “coordination without command” and “unity without uniformity” across differences, so people can build shared understanding, surface common ground, develop proposals, and coordinate reforms and action without requiring domination, collapse, or coercive centralization.

OWA is oriented toward universal applicability and inclusion: it is meant to scale from the smallest human context to a planetary context, so that every person can have a meaningful voice in the decisions that shape their lives on Earth. Its ethical posture is defined as lawful, nonviolent, and continuously evolving cooperation.

## Substrate, not sovereignty

OWA is not a sovereign authority, a centralized government, a “shadow government,” or a command hierarchy. It does not seize authority, does not require permission from existing institutions to exist, and does not depend on state sponsorship to operate.

OWA is designed as a civic substrate rather than a replacement superstructure: a non-coercive layer that stabilizes, informs, and enables reform by making collective human alignment legible and actionable, without requiring immediate replacement of existing institutions. It is explicitly framed as strengthening what works, exposing misalignment, and supporting reform through clarity rather than confrontation.

OWA coordinates people, not governments: it is a bottom-up system that connects participants across boundaries while preserving cultural and political differences. Its influence is intended to emerge from legitimacy, coordination capacity, and the clarity of shared will, not from enforcement power.

## Fractal assemblies as the core pattern

OWA is structurally identical at every scale of human organization. It is composed of assemblies that repeat the same core pattern in different contexts, with no level treated as “symbolic,” “incomplete,” or merely advisory. Each assembly contains full capacity for discussion, coordination, decision-making, and adaptation.

OWA’s canonical structural grammar is simple and invariant: Open [Scale] Assembly of [Place]. Assemblies are formed at whatever geographic resolutions are meaningful (e.g., neighborhood, city, region, nation, world), with the same democratic logic repeated at each level.

OWA is geographic-first. Legitimacy originates in geographically grounded assemblies: real people engaging in real local contexts. Larger-scale coordination emerges from aggregation of signals originating in localities rather than from top-down authority. Geographic grounding is treated as the root layer from which wider coordination becomes credible and resilient.

OWA can include non-geographic coordination structures, teams, nodes, and other abstract groupings, but these operate as overlays. They can organize work, deliberation, and cross-boundary coordination; they do not define or override legitimacy, and they do not replace geographic assemblies as the system’s foundational anchor.

## A single consent signal across scales

OWA is designed to preserve local sovereignty while enabling large-scale coordination. Decisions are made as locally as possible, but the system is built so alignment can propagate upward and outward when broader coordination is relevant. This prevents large-scale decision-making from requiring central intermediaries while avoiding fragmentation into isolated local silos.

OWA’s core scaling mechanic is a one-signal voting principle (described in the project materials as “One-Ballot Multi-Scope”): a participant expresses a decision once locally, and that signal can propagate across relevant scales without requiring duplicate voting at each level, without requiring separate election systems per scope, and without translation loss between levels. This is the mechanism intended to make “local participation → global coordination” structurally possible.

OWA is direct-participation-first. Delegation is not required for scale: the system is explicitly conceived to scale without delegation. Where delegation exists, it is a voluntary bandwidth mechanism, delegated only by choice, issue/scope-specific where appropriate, always revocable, and never allowed to become a permanent ruling caste or a substitute for direct participation.

## How coordination becomes action

Every assembly runs the same invariant decision loop:

**Sense → Think → Act → Learn → Adapt**

This loop defines OWA’s minimal functional completeness: sensing reality and signal, deliberating and forming understanding, converting decisions into commitments and coordinated action, learning from outcomes, and adapting both local practice and broader alignment through iteration.

OWA’s action model is non-coercive by design: action is generated through consent-based alignment and coordinated participation rather than command enforcement. Assemblies act within their context, and cross-assembly coordination is additive rather than mandatory: linkages increase capability and coherence, but an assembly must be able to function meaningfully without depending on global adoption.

OWA’s pathway for reform is defined as “synchronized civic convergence”: distributed and iterative participation that surfaces common ground, develops proposals, and aligns around practical improvements while preserving continuity and reducing the need for destabilizing conflict. As participation grows, the system is intended to become more adaptive and resilient, gaining clarity through use and strengthening over time through iteration.

## Legitimacy, trust, and influence

OWA defines legitimacy as bottom-up and consent-derived: higher-level coordination emerges from the visible aggregation of local consent rather than imposed authority, inherited office, or centralized verification regimes. This makes local grounding not merely a cultural preference but a structural basis for credible global coordination.

OWA treats identity and trust as gradated rather than binary. Trust is conceptualized as probabilistic confidence built over time through evidence, participation history, and relational context, not as a simplistic “verified vs unverified” switch. Influence is therefore not assumed to be uniform: participation is open, while influence can vary by local grounding, demonstrated trust, and relevance to the scope of the decision.

OWA’s trust model is explicitly relational and layered. Stronger proof yields stronger influence especially in local contexts; proof is opt-in and can take multiple forms, including community relationships and local grounding mechanisms rather than centralized identity mandates. The system is designed to resist manipulation by making fake participation expensive and low-impact, emphasizing time, relational credibility, local interaction, and coherence across independent contexts rather than relying on “prevention alone.”

OWA does not require public real-name identity as a precondition for participation. The canon-level invariant is continuity without exposure: a participant can sustain identity continuity over time without needing to place personal safety, employment, or social standing at risk through mandatory public disclosure.

## Memory, transparency, and evolution

OWA requires a durable civic memory: proposals, decisions, votes, and governance history must be preserved in a way that is tamper-evident and auditable, preventing silent history rewriting. This record preserves integrity and enables accountability through transparency; it does not itself confer legitimacy, which remains grounded in consent and real-world participation.

OWA is defined as a living system. It is not a fixed design assumed complete in advance; it is intended to learn, adapt, and evolve through continuous use. The system’s resilience is expected to increase with participation and iteration: clarity improves through engagement, and coordination capacity strengthens as assemblies practice the shared loop and link across contexts.
